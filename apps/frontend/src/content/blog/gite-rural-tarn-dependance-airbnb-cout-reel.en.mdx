Friday evening, 7 p.m. Élodie and Julien close the door of their gîte in Cordes-sur-Ciel, an old stone building they restored over three years before opening it to guests. A couple from Bordeaux has just left after a week at **980 €**. On their monthly Airbnb statement, the platform has taken its share — a 3 % host commission plus guest fees baked into the displayed price. They know it, they accepted it, and yet, every month-end, there’s that quiet dizziness.

What bothers them isn’t really the commission. It’s something else. They don’t know the full name of the couple that left this morning. They don’t have their direct email. They can’t send them a card next year inviting them back in low season. When the guest asked “can we book directly with you next time?”, they had to say no — Airbnb forbids it in its terms of service.

They run a 4-star gîte, a stone’s throw from one of the most beautiful villages in France, and they are tenants of their own customer base.

## Rural tourism in the Tarn has never been more dynamic — but not for everyone

According to Insee and Atout France data, Occitanie remains one of France’s leading tourist regions, and the Tarn has been benefiting for several years from a real surge in rural tourism: demand for wide open spaces, slower trips, short stays by car from Toulouse, Bordeaux or Montpellier. Cordes-sur-Ciel, Penne-du-Tarn, Puycelsi, Monestiés, Lacaune: demand for authentic stays — gîtes, bed and breakfasts, character houses — grows every year.

The paradox is that this growth mostly benefits the big platforms. Airbnb, Booking.com, Abritel capture most of the search traffic, set their own rules, and leave hosts in a position of structural dependence. A gîte that runs 90 % of its nights through Airbnb is not an independent owner — it’s an Airbnb subcontractor.

And that dependency costs far more than the advertised commission.

## The hidden cost of the status quo

The visible commission is easy to calculate. On Airbnb, hosts typically pay around 3 % and guests see added service fees; on Booking.com, the host commission is significantly higher. Across platforms, depending on the model chosen, **the real take sits between 15 and 25 %** of gross revenue — sometimes more once you add commissions, payment fees, sponsored listings and cancellation penalties.

Take Élodie and Julien. Their gîte is booked 28 weeks a year on average, at roughly 900 € per week.

- Gross annual revenue: **25,200 €**
- Platform take (range 15–25 %): **3,780 to 6,300 €/year**
- That is **135 to 225 € per booked week** going out the door

But the visible cost is only the tip. Here’s what almost nobody counts, and what weighs far more over 5 years:

**1. Captive customer data.** Airbnb and Booking keep the emails, phone numbers, booking histories. You can’t build a base of loyal customers. A gîte running for 10 years with 600 bookings has generated **600 guests it cannot contact again**. That’s an email database worth 5,000–10,000 € in lost value, every year.

**2. The opaque algorithm.** Your visibility depends on a ranking you don’t control. A rule change, a lower rating, a new weighting — and your occupancy drops 20 % without warning. No recourse.

**3. Enforced positioning.** On Airbnb you sit next to a 45 €-a-night flat in Toulouse. You can’t tell your story, your terroir, your dry-stone restoration. You’re a thumbnail in a grid of 3,000 results.

**4. Price pressure and free cancellations.** There is constant pressure to lower the displayed price or accept looser terms. Every concession eats into the margin.

**Concrete 5-year calculation** for Élodie and Julien: between **19,000 and 31,500 €** in cumulative commissions, plus a customer base that still isn’t theirs. With a direct booking site capturing even 30 % of reservations direct, net savings easily exceed **1,500 to 2,500 €/year**, and the customer base becomes a real asset.

## The 3 levels of solution

### Level 1 — Landing page + contact form: 0 to 500 €

One page per property, with photos, rates, description, and a form that sends an email. Guests see the availability (manually updated), and you reply to confirm. Payment happens by bank transfer or a Stripe link sent manually.

**What it does:**
- Gives you your own address (e.g. `yourgite-cordes.com`) you can put on your cards, on your Airbnb description, on Instagram
- Lets you collect emails directly (from incoming enquiries)
- Costs almost nothing to maintain

**What it doesn’t do:**
- No automated booking — you confirm each request manually
- No real-time calendar: risk of double booking with the platforms
- No automatic online payment

**For whom:** a small gîte or B&B just starting out, or one renting only around twenty weeks a year, looking to test direct demand without investing.

**Setup:** 1 to 2 days. Hosting around 10 €/month, domain 12 €/year.

### Level 2 — Site + synchronised booking engine: 30 to 80 €/month

This is the level that changes everything for most gîtes. You plug a direct booking engine into the site, with a real-time calendar, automatic card payment (via Stripe or PayPal), and crucially **synchronisation with Airbnb and Booking** through a channel manager. Specialised PMS (Property Management Systems) tools handle this sync to avoid double bookings.

**What it does:**
- A single calendar synced across all platforms
- Direct card payment, automatic deposit, custom cancellation terms
- Guest emails that belong to you (and on which you can build a newsletter)
- Your real rates displayed without added service fees — often cheaper for the traveller than the platform, even with your margin
- The option to state “Book direct, save 10 %” on your Airbnb profile (within each platform’s rules)

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t replace Airbnb visibility overnight — you still need to bring in traffic
- Requires some discipline in pricing and rules management
- Doesn’t handle local SEO on its own — you will need to publish some content

**For whom:** a gîte or B&B already renting more than 20 weeks a year, wanting to take back control of at least 30 % of its bookings.

**Setup:** 1 to 3 weeks. PMS subscription 30 to 80 €/month, Stripe fees 1.4 % + 0.25 € per EU card transaction, configuration cost 1,500 to 3,000 € if outsourced.

### Level 3 — Custom site + CRM + integrated calendar: 3,000 to 8,000 € then 30 €/month

The option for building a **hospitality brand**, not just a listing. A custom site that tells the story of your place, your history, your restoration, your discovery routes around Cordes-sur-Ciel or Penne-du-Tarn. Integrated calendar, CRM to build loyalty, automated newsletter, upsell module for add-on services (table d’hôtes, local produce basket, on-site massage, guided village walk).

**What it does:**
- A unique design that pulls your gîte out of the Airbnb grid
- Built-in calendar and payment, synced with the platforms
- CRM: history of each guest, automatic follow-ups, targeted low-season offers, birthday cards
- Blog or journal for local SEO (“Cordes-sur-Ciel gîte”, “Penne-du-Tarn bed and breakfast”)
- Upsell of extras (baskets, tastings, activities)
- Unified dashboard: bookings, revenue, occupancy rate, guest origin

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Not a short-term investment: ROI shows on an 18–36 month horizon
- Doesn’t let you drop the platforms completely (at least at first) for incoming traffic
- Not relevant for a property renting fewer than 15 weeks a year

**For whom:** ambitious hospitality businesses, 2 to 5 gîtes or a large B&B, aiming to reach 60–70 % direct bookings over 3 years and build a loyal customer base.

**Setup:** 4 to 8 weeks. Design 3,000 to 8,000 € depending on complexity, then 20 to 40 €/month hosting and maintenance.

## Traps to avoid

**Trap 1 — Believing you can quit Airbnb overnight.** Bad idea. Platforms bring real traffic, especially for a guest’s first-ever booking. The right strategy is gradual: keep the platforms for acquisition, shift the second visit direct through your email base and your site.

**Trap 2 — Accepting any channel manager.** Not all booking engines sync equally well. A bad sync = double booking = guaranteed negative review. Choose a tool that really handles Airbnb, Booking and Abritel in real time, not on a 2-hour delay.

**Trap 3 — Forgetting the “Book direct” page.** If your site doesn’t have a clear page saying “by booking here, you save X %, you pay direct, and you get the real owner’s contact”, your visitors will reflexively bounce back to Airbnb. The advantage has to be explicit.

**Trap 4 — Neglecting photos and story.** On your own site you no longer have Airbnb’s calibrated photos to save you. Invest a day in a serious photo shoot and two hours writing the story of your place. Without that, even the best site won’t convert.

## Three questions to ask yourself this weekend

1. **What share of my bookings already comes from returning guests or direct word of mouth?** If it’s more than 15 %, you already have a community that just needs a site to find you directly. If it’s under 5 %, that’s the sign that platforms are capturing your entire customer relationship — which is exactly the problem to fix.

2. **If Airbnb changed its algorithm tomorrow and my occupancy dropped 30 %, how long would my business hold up?** If the answer is “a few months at most”, your dependency is a strategic fragility, not a convenience.

3. **What do I want to build over 10 years?** A gîte that subcontracts its visibility to platforms and whose value depends on an algorithmic ranking? Or a hospitality brand with its own name, loyal guests, its own story? Both choices are legitimate — but they aren’t built with the same tools.
