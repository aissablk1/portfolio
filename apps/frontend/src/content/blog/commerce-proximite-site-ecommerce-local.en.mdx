You run a shop in a town of fewer than 100,000 people. You see your customers ordering online from Amazon. And you wonder: "Should I have an e-commerce site too?"

The short answer: it depends on what you sell, to whom, and how much time you're willing to invest. The long answer is this article.

## Who e-commerce works for — and who should skip it

Let's be honest: an e-commerce site isn't relevant for every local shop. Here's the reality.

**It's worth it if:**
- You sell products with regular turnover (specialty grocery, wine shop, artisan bakery, florist)
- Your customers already ask "Can I order before coming in?"
- You have a catalog of at least 20 to 30 stable references
- You're willing to spend 3 to 5 hours per week on management (stock updates, order preparation)

**It's NOT worth it if:**
- You sell pure services (hairdresser, plumber, fitness coach) — a showcase site + booking system is enough
- Your catalog changes daily with no predictable pattern (antique shop, permanent yard sale)
- You don't have the time or desire to manage online orders
- Your catchment area is 2 km and all your customers walk past your storefront

If you're in the second category, close this article and invest in your Google Business Profile. It's free and it's where 87% of your local customers find you (source: BrightLocal, Local Consumer Review Survey 2025).

## The 3 models of local e-commerce

There isn't just "one" e-commerce. There are three distinct models, with very different costs and complexities.

### Model 1: The showcase catalog (no online payment)

Your product catalog is online, with photos and prices. The customer browses, then visits the shop or calls to order. No payment on the site.

**Advantages:**
- Very low cost (often integrable into an existing showcase site)
- No online logistics management
- Your customers see your products before coming in
- Zero e-commerce dispute risk (no online sale = no withdrawal rights to manage)

**Disadvantages:**
- No direct sales — you lose customers who want to buy immediately
- No abandoned cart to follow up on
- Limited in terms of conversion

**For whom:** Clothing boutiques, home decor shops, independent bookstores — shops where customers like to touch and see before buying.

### Model 2: Click & collect

The customer orders and pays online, then picks up in-store. This is the model that exploded since 2020 and remains the best fit for local retail.

**Advantages:**
- The customer buys whenever they want (evenings, Sundays, during lunch break)
- No shipping costs — the customer comes to you
- Immediate cash flow (payment before pickup)
- Reduces in-store waiting time (the order is already prepared)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires in-store organization (storage space for ready orders, notification process)
- Real-time stock management is mandatory — a product sold in-store and online simultaneously = problem
- Customers expect short turnaround (often 2 to 4 hours for food click & collect)

**For whom:** Bakeries, pastry shops, wine shops, cheese shops, specialty groceries, greengrocers, florists — any shop with recurring products and a loyal local clientele.

### Model 3: Local delivery

The customer orders online and gets delivery within a 10 to 30 km radius. More complex, more expensive, but with higher revenue potential.

**Advantages:**
- You reach customers who wouldn't travel to your shop
- Higher average basket (customers order more when they don't carry bags)
- Differentiation from competitors who don't deliver

**Disadvantages:**
- Delivery cost: between €4.50 and €8 per trip through local subcontracting (Stuart, local courier services) or €0 if you deliver yourself (but that's your time)
- Preparation and packaging logistics
- Delivery slot management
- Risk of breakage or damaged products (especially food)

**For whom:** Wine shops, caterers, specialty groceries with average basket > €35, shops in rural areas with dispersed clientele.

## When NOT to do e-commerce

This section is the most important in the article.

**Don't launch an e-commerce site if:**

1. **You haven't sorted out your basic local visibility.** If your Google Business Profile isn't complete, if you don't have 20 recent reviews, if your showcase website doesn't exist — start there. E-commerce without traffic is an empty shop on a deserted street.

2. **Your margins can't handle the commissions.** If you sell low-margin products (< 25%) with a low average basket (< €20), payment commissions + platform costs eat your profit. An average basket of €15 with 2% commission + €0.25 per transaction = €0.55 in fees. On a margin of €3.75 (25%), that's 15% of your margin going to technical fees.

3. **You have no one to manage orders.** An unattended e-commerce site — orders processed late, stock not updated, customer questions unanswered — destroys your reputation faster than it builds it.

4. **You want to "be online" without a strategy.** An e-commerce site without promotion (local SEO, local Google Ads, social media) generates zero sales. The site alone attracts no one — it's a tool, not a strategy.

## FAQ

### How many sales per month to break even on a local e-commerce site?

With Shopify (€36/month + click & collect app at €29/month = €65/month fixed), you need approximately €260 in monthly sales to cover fixed costs (assuming 25% gross margin). With WooCommerce, the threshold is much lower: about €50 to €70/month in sales is enough to cover hosting. Custom development requires higher volume — expect €800 to €1,200/month in sales to amortize the investment over 2 years.

### Does click & collect work for a shop in a town of 15,000 people?

Yes, and that's often where it's most useful. In a mid-size town, your customers have regular habits but tight schedules. Click & collect lets them order in the morning from the office and pick up at the end of the day. Bakeries and pastry shops that adopted click & collect in towns of 10,000 to 30,000 people see a 12 to 18% revenue increase within 6 months (source: CCI Occitanie, connected commerce study 2025).

### Do I need to sync in-store and online stock?

It's essential. Selling online a product that's no longer on the shelf means a disappointed customer and a guaranteed negative review. Two solutions: either a connected POS system (Lightspeed, SumUp, Zettle) that updates stock in real-time across both channels, or manual management with a safety margin (you list 80% of your actual stock online to absorb discrepancies). Automatic synchronization costs between €30 and €89/month (connected POS subscription), but it eliminates errors.

### My local competitor has an e-commerce site — do I have to build one too?

No. If your competitor has a mediocre e-commerce site with 12 products, blurry photos, and orders processed in 3 days, not having one is better than having a bad one. However, if their site is well-made and they're taking customers who would have bought from you — yes, you're losing ground. The real question isn't "does my competitor have one?" but "do my customers want one?" Ask them directly — an Instagram poll or a question at checkout is enough.
