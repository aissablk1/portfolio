Saturday morning, 9 a.m. Philippe is in the small back office of an amateur rugby club in Graulhet, in the Tarn. In front of him, a stack of 140 paper membership forms, a shoe box full of cheques, and a binder where he copies out by hand, column by column, names, dates of birth and licence numbers. He has been the volunteer chair for three years. He does this every September.

Next to him, his phone buzzes non-stop: three different WhatsApp groups (board members, U12 parents, senior team), a question about Sunday’s kick-off time, a parent asking whether they can pay in instalments, a former member who wants last year’s tax receipt. Philippe sighs. He had planned to spend the afternoon with his grandchildren.

His non-profit has no website. No management tool. No budget. And nobody on the board “who is good with computers”. Philippe tells himself that “that kind of thing is for big associations with public grants”. He is wrong, and it is costing him hundreds of hours every year.

## What the French non-profit sector actually weighs

France has more than one and a half million active loi 1901 non-profits, the vast majority run by volunteers. Insee and the Recherches & Solidarités network, which observe the French non-profit landscape every year, consistently point to two converging facts: the sector carries substantial economic and social weight (several points of GDP, millions of members, a meaningful share of non-market employment), and the first critical resource for small structures is not money — it is volunteer time.

Put differently: every hour a chair or a treasurer spends re-typing paper forms is an hour not spent on the pitch, with members, or looking for partners. And those hours add up fast.

## The hidden cost of the status quo

Take Philippe’s case, chair of a 140-member sports club. Let’s be honest about what he does every season:

- **Paper memberships in September**: 140 forms to enter, 140 cheques to bank, 140 medical certificates to file. Around **25 hours**.
- **Chasing unpaid dues**: individual emails, texts, phone calls. Around **2 hours a month × 10 months = 20 hours**.
- **Chaotic WhatsApp communication**: three groups, repeated questions, schedules posted twice, lost parents. Around **3 hours a week = 120 hours a year**.
- **Event calendar living inside the chair’s head**: tournaments, AGM, galette, raffle, summer camp. Every incoming question is a small fire to put out. Around **1 hour a week = 40 hours a year**.
- **Year-end tax receipts**: for every member who asks, a Word file to open, edit, export to PDF, email. Around **10 hours**.

**Total: 215 hours a year, just for admin.** The equivalent of five full-time working weeks. Five weeks Philippe does not spend with his players, his family, or looking for a sponsor who could fund real kit.

If you valued that time at the loaded French minimum wage, you would be talking about several thousand euros a year. But the real currency here isn’t the euro: it’s volunteer burnout — that silent erosion that leads, year after year, to a chair eventually handing back the keys while nobody wants to pick them up.

## The 3 levels of solution

### Level 1 — 100 % free: from 0 € to almost nothing

The good news is that a non-profit can already do 80 % of the journey without spending a single euro. The ecosystem of open-source and “nonprofit” free tiers is built precisely for this.

**What it does:**
- **Google Workspace for Nonprofits**: full office suite (Gmail with your own domain, Drive, shared Calendar, Meet), free for eligible non-profits who apply. End of documents scattered across personal laptops.
- **HelloAsso**: a French platform, free for the non-profit, that lets you collect memberships, dues, donations and event registrations by card online. The service is funded by an optional tip offered to donors at checkout. Automatic generation of tax receipts and membership certificates.
- **Framaforms** (Framasoft): free open-source forms for registrations, surveys and miscellaneous requests, hosted in France.
- **Framadate**: availability polls to schedule a board meeting or a trip.
- **Mailchimp free tier**: newsletter up to several hundred contacts, more than enough for a small non-profit.
- **A single WhatsApp broadcast channel** or mailing list: clean top-down communication, end of the noise from 80-person groups.

**What it doesn’t do:**
- No public website indexed on Google with the club’s story, address, schedules, match photos. You remain hard to find for a new member.
- No centralised database — you juggle between several tools, each with its own login.
- No unified dashboard: to know how many members are up to date with their dues, you have to cross-check HelloAsso and a spreadsheet.

**For whom:** non-profits with fewer than 100 members, or one just getting started. It’s already a huge qualitative leap compared to paper.

**Setup:** 1 to 3 weekends from a motivated volunteer, helped by a teenager in the club if needed. No recurring cost.

### Level 2 — Micro-budget: 200 to 500 € per year

You add a real public website, a domain name owned by the non-profit, and you start connecting the tools together.

**What it does:**
- **Domain name**: `ourclub.fr` — around 10 to 15 €/year. You exist for real online, and your email addresses become `chair@ourclub.fr` instead of a personal Gmail that disappears when the chair changes.
- **Website on self-hosted WordPress.org** (free open-source CMS) with French shared hosting at 40 to 80 €/year. Standard pages: presentation, team, news, calendar, membership, contact, legal.
- **HelloAsso integration**: a “Join” button that leads directly to your HelloAsso form. End of the paper form and cheque.
- **Public calendar** synced with the board’s shared Google Calendar: everyone sees the same dates, updated only once.
- **Contact form** routed to a single inbox checked by two board members.

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t build itself. A volunteer needs to spend 15 to 25 hours configuring it — or a service provider has to do it.
- Doesn’t replace human interaction: the site informs, HelloAsso cashes in, but parents will still phone for specific questions.
- Requires regular security updates (WordPress, plugins): about 1 hour a month, otherwise the site eventually gets hacked.

**For whom:** non-profits with 100 to 400 members, or any that run several events a year and want them visible.

**Setup:** 2 to 4 weeks on a volunteer basis, or 3 to 7 days if a service provider handles it. All-in annual budget: **200 to 500 €**.

### Level 3 — Custom site and automations: 1,000 to 3,000 € delivered, then light maintenance

For a more structured non-profit, with multiple sections, several hundred members, or that receives public grants, the next level up makes sense.

**What it does:**
- A custom site that actually reflects the club’s identity (not a generic 2012 template that embarrasses the younger members)
- Protected members area: each member accesses their own documents (tax receipt, membership certificate, licence), without bothering the treasurer
- Automatic generation of year-end tax receipts, bulk-sent by email
- Automated reminders for unpaid dues (D+7, D+30, D+60) with a direct payment link
- Unified dashboard: number of members paid up, income, upcoming event registrations
- Integration with HelloAsso, Mailchimp, Google Calendar — one place where everything converges

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t magically turn a non-profit into a company. Board meetings are still needed, AGMs too.
- Not for a 50-member club: too expensive relative to real need.
- Requires a stable point of contact inside the non-profit for maintenance and changes.

**For whom:** non-profits with more than 300 members, local federations, cultural associations running a full event season, mutual-aid groups with weekly drop-ins.

**Setup:** 3 to 6 weeks. Design cost: **1,000 to 3,000 €** delivered, then around **150 to 300 €/year** for hosting and maintenance.

## Traps to avoid

**Trap 1 — Believing you need “someone technical” before you can start.** Many non-profits wait for a “computer-savvy” member to join the board before they move. That person never arrives — or if they do, they leave a year later. The truth is that starting with HelloAsso and a Framaforms form is, in 2026, within reach of anyone who can use a computer one hour a week.

**Trap 2 — Building a site nobody will maintain.** A WordPress site left alone for six months ends up hacked, hijacked for spam, or becomes a liability. Before launching anything, identify **who** will maintain it, and assume that person will leave one day. Document everything, keep credentials in a shared vault (not on a sticky note in the chair’s drawer).

**Trap 3 — Depending on tools that monetise your data.** Some “free” services aggressively monetise member data. For a non-profit, that’s a real legal risk (GDPR) and a moral one. Favour clearly identified French or European providers with readable terms — HelloAsso, Framasoft, self-hosted WordPress.org on a French host are good reflexes.

**Trap 4 — Refusing outside help on principle.** Volunteering is a value, but it has limits. When the chair spends 215 hours a year on admin, accepting 2,000 € from a “digital” grant to have a clean site built frees up 150 hours of useful volunteering. That isn’t betraying the spirit of volunteering — it’s preserving it.

## How much does it really cost

| Item | Level 1 (free) | Level 2 (micro-budget) | Level 3 (custom) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Setup / design | 0 € | 0 to 800 € | 1,000 to 3,000 € |
| Domain + annual hosting | 0 € | 50 to 100 € | 50 to 150 € |
| Tools (HelloAsso, Mailchimp, etc.) | 0 € | 0 to 150 € | 0 to 300 € |
| Annual maintenance | 0 € | 0 to 250 € (volunteer) | 150 to 300 € |
| Weekly management time | 1 to 2 h | 30 min to 1 h | 15 to 30 min |
| Members managed painlessly | 30 to 100 | 100 to 400 | 300 to 2,000 |

**Key point:** moving from paper to Level 1 costs nothing — just one or two weekends. Moving from Level 1 to Level 2 costs the price of a set of jerseys. Moving to Level 3 costs the price of a second-hand trailer. No loi 1901 non-profit has a structural reason to be stuck on paper in 2026.

## Three questions to ask yourself this weekend

1. **How many volunteer hours a year are currently absorbed by pure admin?** If you don’t know, keep a log for two weeks. Reality is usually heavier than how it feels.

2. **Who on the board will be able to take over the digital tools when the current chair leaves?** If the answer is “nobody”, your priority isn’t a new site — it’s clear documentation and shared access.

3. **What’s stopping me from testing HelloAsso on Monday?** 95 % of the time, the answer is “I haven’t carved out an hour to do it”. That one hour is worth a hundred hours saved next September.
