Saturday, 10:15 a.m. Thomas is standing at the entrance of his cellar in Castelnau-de-Montmiral. A couple from Toulouse has just parked in front of the gate. They found the estate typing “Gaillac winery visit” on their phone during morning coffee. Thomas is alone, he has a late harvest to monitor, and nothing is prepared. He improvises a one-hour tour, offers a free tasting, sells four bottles. Time spent: 90 minutes. Revenue: 48 €. Real margin: barely 15 €.

On that same Saturday, three other couples drive past the gate without stopping — they don’t know if the estate is open. Two groups of friends who wanted to book an afternoon tasting gave up because they couldn’t find a phone number. And a Toulouse corporate-events agency has been searching for three weeks for a Gaillac estate for a twenty-person team-building day, with no accessible online contact.

Thomas doesn’t need more visitors. He needs a simple way to turn passers-by into booked guests, and those into profitable experiences.

## Wine tourism is no longer a sideline — it’s a channel of its own

Atout France, the French state operator for tourism, has identified wine tourism as a growth driver for rural tourism for several years. Insee confirms in its tourism surveys that visiting producers is among the motivations of a growing share of travellers in the Occitanie region.

For a Gaillac winery, visitors no longer come just to buy bottles: they come to **experience something**. A walk through the vines, an explanation of the local grapes — Mauzac, Len de l’El, Braucol, Duras — a guided tasting, sometimes an overnight stay. And they are willing to pay. The average spend of a paying wine tourist is several times that of a casual guest stopping by for a free taste.

The problem: if your site doesn’t let them book, you lose most of these visitors before they even decide to come. Wine tourists plan online, often the night before, and pick the estate with the smoothest booking flow — not necessarily the one with the best wine.

## The hidden cost of the status quo

Typical case: a Gaillac family estate, 25 hectares, welcoming **15 groups per week in high season** (April–October) on improvised free visits, average sale 50 € per group. Off-season, 3 groups a week. Over the year: around **12,000 €** of hospitality revenue, for **300 hours** spent.

Now the same estate with online booking: paid visits (15 €/person, tasting included), weekend premium slots (35 €/person, cheese pairings), two privatisable events per month (500 to 800 €):

- **Paid visits**: 15 groups of 4 × 60 € × 28 weeks → **25,200 €**
- **Premium tastings**: 6 people × 35 € × 40 weekends → **8,400 €**
- **Private events**: 2 × 650 € × 10 months → **13,000 €**
- **Cellar sales linked to hospitality** (40 % conversion × 60 €) → **around 15,000 €**

Total: **around 61,000 €** of annual hospitality revenue versus 12,000 € today. Difference: **+49,000 €/year**, with reduced management time because each visit is framed. This isn’t optimistic: it’s what Gaillac estates that have structured their hospitality already achieve. The status quo literally costs you tens of thousands of euros a year.

## The 3 levels of solution

### Level 1 — Landing page + booking form: free to 300 €

Minimal but effective: a dedicated visit page with hours, prices, description, photos and a form that emails each enquiry. Scheduling via Google Calendar or Calendly (15 €/month for the paid version).

**What it does:**
- 24/7 booking without inbound calls
- Clear pricing displayed — no more undervalued tastings
- Enquiries centralised in one inbox
- Visibility on Google for “Gaillac winery visit”

**What it doesn’t do:**
- No online payment — no-show risk remains
- No automatic slot management (double-booking possible)
- No post-visit automatic follow-up
- Each confirmation handled manually

**For whom:** a winery welcoming fewer than 10 groups per week that wants to test paid wine tourism without investing.

**Setup:** 2 to 4 days of work. A Carrd site (19 €/year) or a WordPress page is enough. Free Calendly for the first bookings.

### Level 2 — Dedicated booking platform: 40 to 100 €/month

You add a real booking engine with integrated online payment. Generalist platforms like Bookeo or Regiondo, designed for tourism activities, automatically manage slots, groups, payments and cancellations. Shopify or WooCommerce with a booking add-on also works, if you want to combine shop and visits in one tool.

**What it does:**
- Online booking with card payment (deposit or full)
- Real-time calendar, no more double-booking
- Automatic confirmation and day-before reminder emails
- Group, schedule, language management
- Post-visit follow-ups (review, bottle offer)
- Attendance stats

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t showcase your estate identity — you stay in a template
- Anglo-Saxon tools ignore French wine tax specifics
- You still drive the traffic (SEO, social)

**For whom:** estates welcoming between 10 and 40 groups per week in season, looking to professionalise hospitality and limit no-shows.

**Setup:** 1 to 3 weeks. Budget 40 to 100 €/month subscription depending on the platform, plus 1.4 to 2.9 % transaction fees. 800 to 2,500 € if you outsource the setup and integration with your existing website.

### Level 3 — Custom site with wine tourism CRM: 4,000 to 9,000 € then 30 €/month

For estates that want to **build an experience**, not just sell visits. A custom website lets you tell the estate’s story, offer several formats (standard tour, blending workshop, premium tasting, privatisation, overnight stays if you run guest rooms), and manage the full customer relationship from one dashboard.

**What it does:**
- Unique design faithful to the estate’s identity — not a template
- Multiple visit and tasting formats, each on its page
- Online booking with payments, deposits, cancellations, reminders
- Built-in CRM: history, preferences, return frequency
- Event module for private bookings (weddings, seminars)
- Estate blog for local SEO (“Gaillac winery visit”, “organic Gaillac tasting”)
- Segmented newsletter (past visitors, loyals, event prospects)
- Unified dashboard: bookings, payments, occupancy, revenue

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t manage itself: content, newsletter, reviews to run
- ROI on 12–24 months, not a short-term investment
- Not for estates under 200 visitors a year

**For whom:** a winery that makes hospitality a core pillar, wants to move upmarket, privatise, attract groups and seminars, build a recognisable brand across the Toulouse region and beyond.

**Setup:** 5 to 10 weeks. Design between 4,000 and 9,000 € depending on complexity (formats, multilingual, PMS integration for guest rooms), then 20 to 40 €/month hosting and maintenance.

## Traps to avoid

**Trap 1 — Relying only on third-party platforms.** Many wineries lean on TripAdvisor, Google Business Profile or regional tourism listings. Useful for visibility, but you own neither the relationship, nor the data, nor any follow-up ability. Your site is the only place where you turn a one-off visitor into a loyal customer who returns every year — and brings their friends.

**Trap 2 — Offering the tasting free as a matter of principle.** A French cultural trap that costs dearly. Foreign visitors are used to paying everywhere in the world, and French visitors understand it perfectly when offered a framed experience (welcome, grapes, pairing, vineyard walk). Charging 15 to 35 € doesn’t scare real enthusiasts — it filters out the curious who wouldn’t have bought anything anyway.

**Trap 3 — Underestimating multilingual needs.** A significant share of wine tourists in Occitanie are international (UK, Netherlands, Belgium, Germany, United States), with strong purchasing power and bookings weeks in advance. An English version, even simple, makes the difference. Machine translations aren’t enough.

**Trap 4 — Forgetting mobile.** Wine tourism bookings happen overwhelmingly on phones, often on the move (café, car, campsite). Without an immediately visible booking button on mobile, you lose half your conversions. Many winery sites are still designed “desktop-first.”

## Three questions to ask yourself this weekend

1. **How many visitors come through each week in high season, and how many actually pay for their visit?** If the ratio is below 30 %, you’re giving away an experience with real value — massive lost revenue.

2. **Do you know how much time you spend on hospitality per euro earned?** If not, hospitality isn’t being managed. A booking system gives that visibility within 3 months.

3. **If a group of 15 wanted to privatise your cellar next Saturday, how would they contact you and how fast would you reply with a priced offer?** The answer is usually the best indicator of your hospitality setup.
