Thursday morning, 7:30 a.m. Marie is in her cellar in Cahuzac-sur-Vère, deep in the Gaillac appellation. She has just received an email: her broker confirms the pickup of 3,000 bottles at **4.20 €** each. She knows those same bottles will be resold at a wine merchant for **9.50 €** and reach the end customer at around **12 €**. The broker, the retailer, and the online platform that will follow share **7.80 € per bottle** — around 65 % of the final price.

Marie produced the bottle. She pruned, harvested, vinified, aged. And she takes home 35 % of the final value.

She would like to sell more directly. But she doesn’t have a website — just a Facebook page updated twice a year, and a Saturday stand at the Gaillac market. She tells herself that “a proper website costs at least 5,000 €” and that “nobody buys wine online from a small producer anyway.”

Both beliefs are wrong. Here’s why.

## What the middlemen really take from you

On a Gaillac bottle sold at 12 € retail to the end customer, here is the typical value split in the indirect channel (ranges based on FranceAgriMer data and observations from the Gaillac Wine Interprofession):

- **Winemaker**: 3.50 to 4.50 € (production cost included)
- **Broker / négociant**: 0.80 to 1.20 €
- **Logistics + storage**: 0.30 to 0.60 €
- **Retailer or platform margin**: 2.50 to 4.00 €
- **VAT**: 2.00 €

You take home roughly **33 to 38 % of the final price**. In direct sales — farmers’ markets, on-site cellar purchases, your own website — you keep **70 to 80 %** of the final price, because you capture both the retailer’s and the broker’s margins.

**Concrete calculation for a 10-hectare estate producing 60,000 bottles a year:**

- 100 % indirect at 4 €/bottle → **240,000 € annual revenue**
- 30 % direct at 10 €/bottle + 70 % indirect at 4 €/bottle → **348,000 € annual revenue**
- Difference: **+108,000 €/year** for exactly the same production

This isn’t an optimistic scenario. It’s basic arithmetic applied to a 30/70 mix — which many Gaillac estates already reach by combining cellar sales, markets, trade fairs and a website.

## The real obstacle isn’t the website price — it’s the belief that “nobody buys online”

The annual SOWINE / Dynata barometer, which has tracked French wine purchase behaviour for more than a decade, shows a clear trend: the share of French consumers who buy wine online at least occasionally grows every year, and it’s significantly higher among under-45s than older buyers. Average baskets observed in direct-producer sales typically sit between 60 and 100 € — roughly 5 to 8 bottles per order.

Online buyers are looking for three things, in this order:

1. **An authentic story** — who makes the wine, where, how
2. **Proof of quality** — awards, medals, press coverage, photos of the estate
3. **Purchase convenience** — 2-click payment, reliable delivery, option to pick up at the cellar

That is exactly what you can offer. A broker cannot tell your story. A generalist platform drowns your estate in a catalogue of 3,000 references. **Your own website is the only place where you are the only brand visible.**

## The 3 levels of direct online sales

### Level 1 — Form + payment: free to 200 €

The simplest option: one page per cuvée, with description, photo, price, and an order form that sends an email. Payment happens by bank transfer, cheque, or via a Stripe or SumUp link sent manually after confirmation.

**What it does:**
- Lets you receive orders 24/7
- Centralises enquiries in one place (no more “I’ll call you back”)
- Builds an email list of customers who have bought (gold for retention)

**What it doesn’t do:**
- No automated cart (you handle each order manually)
- No real-time stock management — you risk selling a cuvée already sold out
- No automatic shipping (you ship yourself or use a local carrier)

**For whom:** independent winemakers selling fewer than 5,000 bottles a year direct, or those just starting out who want to test online interest without investing.

**Setup:** 1 to 2 days of work. A simple HTML site or a platform like Carrd (19 €/year) is enough. Payment via Stripe (1.4 % + 0.25 € per EU card transaction, no subscription).

### Level 2 — Specialised wine e-commerce: 30 to 80 €/month

This is the right level for an estate that wants to automate without breaking the bank. Generalist e-commerce platforms (Shopify, WooCommerce on WordPress) cover a winemaker’s needs very well with an adapted theme. For estates that want a tool built specifically for wine, the historical solution Vin65 has been absorbed into WineDirect, now the reference platform for wine e-commerce worldwide — though it’s more North America–oriented.

**What it does:**
- Automated catalogue with real-time stock tracking
- Direct card payment (no more chasing bank transfers)
- Automatic shipping cost calculation based on weight and destination
- Integration with standard carriers (Chronopost, Colissimo, DPD, GLS, Geodis) with automatic cost calculation
- Automated accounting export

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t sell for you: you still need to drive traffic (SEO, social media, email)
- No personalised story — you’re inside a template, not a bespoke narrative
- “Viticulture” themes all look alike; visual differentiation is hard

**For whom:** estates selling 5,000 to 30,000 bottles a year direct, with e-commerce growth goals.

**Setup:** 1 to 2 weeks. Budget 30 to 80 €/month subscription + 2.9 % Stripe transaction fees. No upfront cost if you do it yourself — 1,500 to 3,000 € if you outsource the configuration.

### Level 3 — Custom site + CRM + integrated wine tourism: 3,000 to 8,000 € then 30 €/month

This is the option for estates that want to build a **brand**, not just a shop. A custom website lets you tell your story, showcase your terroir, integrate a cellar visit booking system, and manage your entire customer relationship from a single tool.

**What it does:**
- A unique design that tells your story — not a template
- Built-in e-commerce (same features as Level 2)
- Wine tourism module: tasting bookings, cellar visits, on-site events
- Integrated CRM: purchase history per customer, automatic follow-ups, birthdays, targeted offers
- Estate blog or journal for your local SEO (“Gaillac winemaker”, “organic Tarn estate”)
- Automated newsletter for retention
- Unified dashboard: orders, stock, bookings, revenue

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t maintain itself: you need to publish content regularly, or have someone publish for you
- Not a short-term investment: ROI shows on a 12–24 month horizon
- Not for estates selling fewer than 500 bottles a year direct — too expensive for the volume

**For whom:** ambitious estates aiming to grow from 30 % to 60 % direct sales over 3 years, or those already welcoming visitors and wanting to structure their wine tourism.

**Setup:** 4 to 8 weeks. Design cost between 3,000 and 8,000 € depending on complexity, then 20 to 40 €/month hosting and maintenance.

## Traps to avoid

**Trap 1 — Believing export is the way out.** Many winemakers turn to international markets expecting salvation. But indirect export brings you back to the same 35 % as domestic indirect sales, with added logistics and customs pain. **Direct export** (via your own site, B2C into Europe) is interesting — indirect export almost never is.

**Trap 2 — Paying to list on a wine marketplace.** Dozens of platforms (Vinatis, iDealwine, Cavissima, Plus de Bulles) offer to list your estate for a 15 to 25 % commission. You end up back in the indirect model, this time with a digital layer on top. It’s rarely profitable mid-term, and you build no customer relationship — the marketplace keeps the data.

**Trap 3 — Launching a site without a traffic plan.** A site with no visitors sells nothing. Before building the shop, ask yourself: **how will people find me?** Local SEO (“organic Gaillac winemaker”), social media (Instagram works very well for wine), existing customer email list, local press, restaurant partnerships. The website is where the sale closes — not where it starts.

**Trap 4 — Underestimating logistics.** Shipping wine isn’t like shipping books. Fragile parcel, heavy weight, licences to respect (no sales to minors, no delivery to Saudi Arabia, etc.). A 6-bottle carton weighs 8 kg — at 12 € shipping cost, you’re already cutting into your margin. You need to absorb shipping into the price or offer free delivery above a threshold (e.g. 12 bottles).

Realistic ranges for a Gaillac estate in 2026:

|---|---|---|---|
| Setup / design | 0 to 500 € | 1,500 to 3,000 € | 3,000 to 8,000 € |
| Monthly subscription | 0 to 20 € | 30 to 80 € | 20 to 40 € |
| Transaction fees | 1.4 % + 0.25 € | 2.9 % + 0.25 € | 1.4 to 2.9 % |
| Weekly management time | 2 to 4 h | 1 to 2 h | 30 min to 1 h |
| Direct sales capacity | 2,000 to 5,000 bottles/year | 5,000 to 20,000 bottles/year | 10,000 to 50,000 bottles/year |

**Level 3 break-even point** (custom site): counting an extra 6 € margin per bottle sold direct instead of indirect, you need to sell about **1,000 additional bottles per year direct** to amortise a 6,000 € site in 12 months. That’s within reach for most estates.

## Three questions to ask yourself this weekend

1. **What share of my current revenue comes from direct sales?** If it’s below 20 %, there’s an obvious upside. If it’s already above 50 %, the next lever is likely wine tourism and retention, not just the shop.

2. **How many loyal customers do I know by first name?** If you know 50, you already have a community waiting to be activated. If you know 500, you have a business that can double in a year just by writing to them.

3. **What’s stopping me from starting this month?** Time? Budget? Fear of doing it wrong? Every answer deserves to be examined. Most of the time, the real block isn’t technical — it’s the absence of someone who knows both wine and the web.
