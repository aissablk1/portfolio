Monday morning, 7am. You're on a job site in Gaillac. Your team leader calls: "Where are we supposed to be today?" The schedule is on a sticky note. On your desk. In Albi.

Result: 45 minutes lost reorganizing the day over the phone. The team arrives late. The client is unhappy. And you've already lost control of your week before 8am.

According to the French Building Federation (FFB), nearly 40% of construction projects run behind the initial schedule. For a tradesperson with 3 to 10 employees, these delays aren't just an inconvenience — they mean contractual penalties (0.1 to 0.3% of the project amount per day of delay), idle crews, and clients who won't come back.

## What paper scheduling really costs you

A construction tradesperson spends an average of 8 to 12 hours per week on admin tasks (source: ArtisanSmart, 2025). Site scheduling accounts for 3 to 4 hours of that total: assigning teams, checking availability, managing delays, notifying clients.

**Real calculation for a tradesperson with 6 employees:**

- 3h/week on scheduling x EUR 45/h (tradesperson hourly cost) = EUR 135/week
- Over a year (47 working weeks): **EUR 6,345 of unbilled time**
- Not counting indirect costs: a 5-day delay on a EUR 25,000 project means EUR 250 to EUR 750 in penalties

The most common problems:

- **Double-booked teams**: two jobs scheduled the same day for the same crew. You find out the night before
- **Forgotten client reminders**: the client was supposed to provide site access on Tuesday, nobody told them. Your crew shows up to a locked door
- **Unplanned absences**: an employee calls in sick, and your entire weekly schedule collapses because there's no overview
- **Uncoordinated subcontractors**: the plasterer arrives before the electrician has finished. Downtime. Real cost

## 3 levels of site scheduling automation

### Level 1: Shared Google Calendar — free

The first step, and often the most effective for a tradesperson with 1 to 3 employees.

**What it does:**
- A shared calendar visible to the entire team on their phones
- Color coding by job site or by team
- Automatic notifications the night before and in the morning
- Real-time sync — if you move a job, everyone sees it instantly

**What it doesn't do:**
- No Gantt chart (no visualization of task dependencies)
- No link to your quotes or invoices
- No resource management (equipment, vehicles)
- No structured job history

**Best for:** solo tradesperson or team of 2-3, with fewer than 5 concurrent jobs.

**Setup time:** 30 minutes. Create a dedicated Google Calendar called "Job Sites", share it with your team. Done.

When you're managing 5 to 15 concurrent jobs with 3 to 10 employees, Google Calendar falls short. You need a Gantt view, team management, and ideally a link between your schedule and your quotes.

For a tradesperson with 5 to 15 employees who wants a system tailored to their workflow — not the other way around.

**What's included:**

- Weekly automated report: active projects, delays, next week's availability
- Integration with your existing quoting/invoicing software (Obat, EBP, Excel)

**Setup time:** 5 to 15 days depending on complexity.

## The quote-to-schedule link: the real time saver

The biggest lever isn't the schedule itself. It's the connection between your schedule and your quotes.

When a client signs a quote, what happens today? You receive the signed quote by email. You open your schedule (paper, Excel, or software). You look for a slot. You assign a team. You tell the team. You confirm the date with the client.

5 manual steps. 20 to 30 minutes per job.

With an automated system:
1. The client signs the quote electronically
2. The job is automatically created in the schedule with the estimated duration (calculated from the quote line items)
3. The system suggests available slots based on which teams are free
4. You validate with one click
5. The client and team receive an automatic confirmation

From 30 minutes down to 2 minutes. Over 10 jobs per month, that's 4h40 recovered.

## FAQ

### Does a solo tradesperson need scheduling software?

No, not paid software. Google Calendar is more than enough for a solo tradesperson with 3 to 5 concurrent jobs. The real need appears when you're managing teams and subcontractors — it's the multi-person coordination that makes paper scheduling impossible.

### My team isn't comfortable with technology. What should I do?

Start with Google Calendar on their phones. Everyone knows how to use a calendar on their phone. No training needed. If you later move to a SaaS, construction-focused tools like Obat and Vertuoza are designed for tradespeople — not IT professionals. Included training sessions last 1 to 2 hours.

### Do financial aid programs cover scheduling software?

In France, yes. The Pass Occitanie covers 50% of digitalization expenses (cap EUR 10,000). OCCAL covers up to 70% (cap EUR 23,000). A custom system at EUR 3,000 could cost you just EUR 900 to 1,500 after aid. Monthly SaaS subscriptions are generally not covered — only one-time investments qualify.

### How long until I see a return on investment?

For a SaaS at EUR 50/month: if you save 2h/week at EUR 45/h, the ROI is immediate (EUR 360 monthly saving vs EUR 50 cost). For a custom system at EUR 3,000: with EUR 135/week in savings, the system pays for itself in 5 to 6 months. Not counting the late penalties avoided and the additional jobs you can take on thanks to the time freed up.
