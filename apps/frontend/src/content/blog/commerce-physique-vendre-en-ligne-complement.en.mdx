Tuesday morning, 10:15. Clara opens her home decor shop on a pedestrian street in the historic center of Albi. She unpacks yesterday’s deliveries — ceramics from a potter in Cordes, three brass light fittings, a Berber rug she picked up at a flea market. At 10:30, a customer pushes the door open: “Hi, I was looking for your address on Google, I wanted to check whether you were open on Mondays… I couldn’t find it.”

Clara smiles, answers, but something nags at her. Because she knows that this particular customer did come in. She doesn’t know how many others, the day before, searched “home decor Albi” on their phones, found nothing clear, and went somewhere else. She also knows she holds about 400 references in stock, that her loyal customers regularly ask her “do you still have that green vase?”, and that she loses ten minutes on every call checking the back room.

Clara tells herself she should “make a website.” A friend mentioned Shopify, her accountant advised against it “because it’s time-consuming,” and a sales rep offered her an e-commerce site for 4,800 €. She no longer knows what to think. The truth is that none of those three answers is right for her.

## What customers really look for before walking in

Since 2020, French purchasing behaviour has shifted for good. Sector studies published by the Fevad (French e-commerce and distance selling federation) and the figures from France Num point to the same trend: before stepping into a physical shop, a growing share of consumers first check opening hours, location, product availability, and sometimes prices online. The purchase journey is no longer “physical” or “digital” — it’s hybrid.

What these people are looking for, in this order:

1. **Does this shop still exist, and is it open right now?** — up-to-date hours, recent photos, reviews
2. **Do they have what I want?** — a few hero products visible, an identifiable style
3. **How do I get there?** — exact address, parking, access

For 90 % of local shops, answering these three questions is already 80 % of the work. The bad news is that a full e-commerce site doesn’t answer them any better than a well-maintained Google Business Profile listing. The good news is that this first level is free.

## The hidden cost of doing nothing

Take Clara’s case. Her decor shop runs at around 180,000 € in annual revenue. She has no website, no up-to-date Google listing, and no browsable catalogue.

**Conservative estimate of weekly lost revenue:**

- **Passers-by who don’t come in** because they have no idea what’s inside: 3 to 5 per day × 6 days × 45 € average basket × 20 % conversion rate = **160 to 270 €/week**
- **“Do you still have…?” calls** poorly handled, customers who don’t call back: 2 to 3 lost baskets/week × 60 € = **120 to 180 €/week**
- **Regulars who would have ordered between visits**, for a gift or a special occasion: 1 to 2 baskets/week × 55 € = **55 to 110 €/week**

Low range: **335 €/week**. High range: **560 €/week**. That’s between **17,000 and 29,000 €/year** in evaporated revenue — on a shop that does 180,000 €. It’s not a catastrophe, but it’s 10 to 16 % of sales walking out the door.

The most frustrating part: nearly all of that upside can be recovered **without e-commerce**. Just with a clean online storefront properly listed locally.

## The 3 levels of online presence for a physical shop

### Level 1 — Online storefront without payment: 0 to 300 €

The real first step, often underestimated. A complete (and free) Google Business Profile listing, plus a very simple one- to five-page website — or even a single well-built page.

**What it does:**
- Shows up-to-date hours, photos, address, phone number, Google reviews
- Presents around fifteen hero products with photos and descriptions
- Highlights the universe, the story, the brands stocked
- Answers common questions (click & collect, parking, gift cards)
- Captures local search traffic (“home decor Albi,” “florist Castres,” “bookshop Gaillac”)

**What it doesn’t do:**
- No cart, no online payment — the customer has to come in or call
- No real-time stock — you feature products, you don’t show a full catalogue
- No automated shipping

**For whom:** 80 % of local shops. Independent bookshops, florists, delicatessens, decor shops, concept stores, wine shops, clothing boutiques with enough foot traffic.

**Setup:** 2 to 5 days of work. Google Business Profile: free, about 2 hours to fill in properly. Storefront site: on a standard host, between 0 and 300 € setup if you use a clean template, or 800 to 1,500 € if you want something polished and bespoke.

### Level 2 — E-commerce shop with click & collect: 30 to 80 €/month

This is the right level for a shop with a stable catalogue, a genuine appetite to sell between visits, and customers comfortable ordering online. Shopify and WooCommerce (on WordPress) cover the vast majority of needs. PrestaShop remains an option for those who want a French-rooted ecosystem. Payment goes through Stripe or SumUp.

**What it does:**
- Browsable full catalogue, with photos and stock
- Cart and card payment online
- **Click & collect**: the customer pays online and picks up in store (zero shipping cost, guarantees a visit)
- Local delivery option (by bike, car, on foot within a 10 km radius)
- Postal shipping available for more distant customers
- Accounting export, promotions, gift cards

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t update itself: you need to photograph, publish, track stock
- Doesn’t sell for you: without traffic (Google, social, emails to loyal customers), a site sells nothing
- Doesn’t replace in-store advice — complementary, not a substitute

**For whom:** shops with 100 to 2,000 relatively stable references, average basket above 30 €, and a shopkeeper willing to spend 2 to 4 hours a week on upkeep.

**Setup:** 1 to 3 weeks. Shopify: around 30 €/month. WooCommerce: 10 to 20 €/month hosting, plus a few plugins. Stripe: 1.4 % + 0.25 € per EU card transaction. Configuration: 0 € if you do it yourself, 1,200 to 2,500 € if you have it done properly.

### Level 3 — Custom site with POS-synchronised stock: 4,000 to 9,000 € then 30 €/month

This is the option for shops with several locations, or a large catalogue (2,000+ references), or a computerised POS whose stock must stay synchronised in real time between shop and website. It’s also the option for building a real local brand, with content, loyalty, and geo-targeted marketing.

**What it does:**
- Real-time stock sync between POS and website (no more “sold in store 10 minutes ago”)
- Unified loyalty program across shop and website
- CRM with purchase history, personalised follow-ups, birthdays
- Events module: workshops, launches, customer evenings
- Local-SEO blog (“birth gift Albi,” “concept store Tarn”)
- Automated newsletters, geo-targeted campaigns
- Unique design that tells the shop’s story

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Not a short-term investment: payback over 12 to 24 months
- Not suited to a shop that hasn’t already proven flow at Level 2
- Requires ongoing content commitment to work

**For whom:** mature shops, revenue above 300,000 €, multiple locations or strong ambition, a team of at least 2 people to feed the site.

**Setup:** 5 to 10 weeks depending on integrations. Budget 4,000 to 9,000 € for design, then 20 to 40 €/month hosting and maintenance, plus possible POS connectors (50 to 150 €/month depending on the POS software).

## Traps to avoid

**Trap 1 — Trying to compete with Amazon.** The most common mistake. An independent bookseller will never win on price, delivery speed or catalogue size. What they win on is curation, advice, universe. Your website should reflect what makes you unique — not attempt to imitate a marketplace. If your strategy is “I want to sell to strangers across the whole country,” you’ll spend a lot to discover that national SEO is a full-time job.

**Trap 2 — Over-investing before you have time to feed the site.** I’ve seen shopkeepers pay 5,000 € for an e-commerce site they never photographed. Three months later, the homepage still shows a “spring collection” in the middle of summer, with products long out of stock. An untended e-commerce site is worse than no site at all: it signals abandonment. Simple rule: if you don’t have 2 to 4 hours a week to spend on it, stay at Level 1.

**Trap 3 — Forgetting Google Business Profile.** It’s the most profitable, most underused tool. Up-to-date listing, fresh photos every month, responses to reviews, regular posts. For a large share of local searches (“florist Lavaur,” “deli Gaillac”), Google displays Business Profile listings before websites. A shop with a perfectly maintained listing and no website can beat a shop with a beautiful site and an empty listing.

**Trap 4 — Confusing local delivery with national shipping.** Delivering by bike within 5 km of Albi for 5 €: realistic, distinctive, it builds loyalty. Shipping a bouquet or a ceramic piece to Lille via a national carrier: a logistical headache that eats into your margin and exposes you to breakage. Always start local: your customers are 10 minutes on foot, not across the country.

## How much does it really cost

Realistic ranges for a local shop in the Tarn in 2026:

| Item | Level 1 | Level 2 | Level 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Setup / design | 0 to 1,500 € | 1,200 to 2,500 € | 4,000 to 9,000 € |
| Monthly subscription | 0 to 15 € | 30 to 80 € | 50 to 200 € (with POS connector) |
| Transaction fees | — | 1.4 to 2.9 % | 1.4 to 2.9 % |
| Weekly time | 30 min to 1 h | 2 to 4 h | 4 to 8 h |
| Additional sales capacity | 5 to 15 % of revenue | 10 to 25 % of revenue | 20 to 40 % of revenue |

**Level 2 break-even point**: on a 180,000 € shop, generating 10 % additional revenue means 18,000 €/year. A 2,500 € e-commerce site is paid back in less than 6 months if the click & collect loop works. The main lever is almost never distance selling — it’s extra sales to existing customers who finally know what’s in stock.

## Three questions to ask yourself this weekend

1. **Is my Google Business Profile listing up to date this week?** Hours, photos, replied-to reviews, a recent post. If the answer is no, that’s where every online project begins — and it’s free.

2. **How many customers ask me each week “do you still have…?” or “when are you open?”** Every question asked is a question your site could answer. If you get 10 such calls a week, you already have proof that an online storefront would help.

3. **Do I have 2 hours a week to devote to a website?** Be honest. If the answer is no, don’t jump to Level 2 — stay at Level 1 and invest those 2 hours in Google Business Profile and Instagram. The worst website is the one you don’t feed.
