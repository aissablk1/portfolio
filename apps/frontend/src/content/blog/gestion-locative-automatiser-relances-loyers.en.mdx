Monday morning, 8:45 a.m. Sandrine arrives at her small office in central Albi, a few steps from the cathedral. She manages 40 units for about ten private landlords scattered between Albi, Gaillac and Castres. She opens her Excel spreadsheet “rent tracking — April”, cross-checks it with yesterday’s bank entries, and marks six lines in red. Six late rents out of 40 — nothing unusual for the start of the month, but six cases to handle by hand.

She already knows how her week will go: two half-days on the phone, writing emails, rereading old exchanges so she isn’t too harsh with the tenant in the small Lapanouse flat who lost his job in February, and not too lenient with the one who “forgets” systematically every quarter. In total, nearly two days a month on reminders alone.

The worst part: she also knows she misses some. A file that slips through is sometimes three months late before she notices — and at that point, the relationship is already damaged and the procedure becomes heavy.

## A late rent is almost never a dishonest tenant

Data published by FNAIM and field reports from UNIS show that the vast majority of rent arrears in the well-managed French private rental market come from life accidents — divorce, illness, job loss, gaps between the end of a fixed-term contract and the start of a new one. Bad-faith cases exist but are a small minority. Insee also confirms that tenant households are structurally more exposed to income shocks than owner-occupiers.

That framing changes everything for whoever handles reminders: the first reminder is not a summons, it’s a hand extended. If your process starts with a cold email — “your rent has not been received, please regularise immediately” — you already lose half of what could have been settled with a single phone call.

The French legal framework for unfurnished rentals is still the 6 July 1989 Act (known as the Mermaz Act), which strictly governs landlord-tenant relations. Beyond a certain level of lateness, litigation (formal notice to pay, termination clause, court summons) is the domain of lawyers and judicial officers. **Everything that follows concerns the amicable phase: roughly day 1 to day 30. Beyond that, it’s no longer automation, it’s law.**

## The hidden cost of doing it all by hand

Back to Sandrine. 40 units, two days a month on reminders, about **16 hours per month** dedicated to that task alone. Valued at 45 €/hour (equivalent billable time for a property manager), that’s **720 €/month** of admin time, or **8,640 €/year**. For a firm that charges 7 to 8 % management fees, that’s the equivalent of the margin on several full units.

On top of that sits an invisible cost:

- **Late payments that snowball** because a file slipped through. A rent that isn’t reminded at day 3 becomes a day-30 case, then a 3-month dispute — with judicial officer fees, long proceedings and often a partial write-off.
- **The relationship with the landlord degrades** as they see their rent arriving late and call the manager to ask “where do we stand?”. Every such call is 15 minutes and a bit of trust eroded.
- **The diffuse stress** of the manager who constantly carries, in her head, “who owes what, since when”. It’s not billable time, but it’s mental load that eats into the rest of the business.

**For a firm managing 40 units, the real cost of manual rent chasing easily reaches 10,000 to 15,000 € per year**, combining time, worsening arrears and damaged relationships. That’s more than the annual cost of any tool on the market.

## The 3 levels of reminder automation

### Level 1 — Excel + Word + manual reminders: 0 to 100 €

The starting point for many small firms. A tracking spreadsheet, Word templates for reminder letters, and calendar pings in Outlook or Google Calendar.

**What it does:**
- Centralises rents and due dates
- Lets you personalise every letter manually
- Near-zero cost, no vendor dependency

**What it doesn’t do:**
- No automatic reminders — you are your own alarm
- No automatic bank reconciliation (you re-enter every transfer)
- No centralised history per tenant (exchanges scatter across your inbox)
- No reliable paper trail if a dispute arises

**For whom:** landlords with 2 or 3 units, a manager just starting out, or a firm handling fewer than 10 units. Beyond that, the risk of missing a file becomes structural.

**Setup:** a few hours to structure a solid spreadsheet and 3 to 4 letter templates (soft reminder, formal reminder, formal notice drafted by a professional). Cost: your own time, plus a Microsoft 365 licence if you don’t already have one.

### Level 2 — Dedicated rental management software: 15 to 60 €/month per user

The right compromise for most independent firms. Platforms such as **Rentila** (French publisher, freemium then paid based on unit count) or **Smovin** (originally Belgian, available in France) cover lease management, rent collection, bank reconciliation, automatic receipts and configurable reminders.

**What it does:**
- Automatic or semi-automatic bank reconciliation: the tool knows who paid without re-entry
- Configurable automated reminders (email at day 3, email at day 10, letter at day 30)
- Centralised history per tenant and per unit
- Automatic generation of receipts and due-date notices
- Landlord dashboard: each owner sees their units in real time
- Electronic signature of leases through integrations (Yousign, DocuSign)

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t finely personalise tone based on the tenant’s human history (the day-3 email is the same for everyone)
- Doesn’t handle the landlord relationship beyond basic reporting: no real landlord CRM
- Doesn’t draft the formal notice itself: you remain in charge of litigation steps, and that’s a good thing
- Doesn’t replace a lawyer or judicial officer for files that escalate

**For whom:** independent firms managing 10 to 100 units, solo or two-person managers, active landlords with 5+ units.

**Setup:** 2 to 5 days to configure, import leases and connect the bank account. Budget 15 to 60 €/month depending on unit volume and features (accounting module, e-signature, etc.).

### Level 3 — Custom platform with landlord CRM and progressive reminders: 4,000 to 12,000 € then 30 to 60 €/month

The option for a firm that has outgrown standard software and wants a tool that reflects its own method. A custom platform is a management tool that bakes in **your** process, **your** templates, **your** reminder rules — and that talks to your accounting, calendar and bank.

**What it does:**
- Progressive reminders **personalised** by tenant profile: a tenant who has been in place for 5 years without a single late payment isn’t treated like a file already in dispute three times
- Full landlord CRM: each owner has a dashboard, history, contact preferences and alerts
- Conditional reminder scenarios: if the tenant clicks the payment link after the first email, no second reminder. If they don’t respond, automatic but gradual escalation
- Automatic generation of formal letter drafts, **to be reviewed and validated by a human** before sending
- Tenant portal (online payment, history, request for a payment plan)
- Integration with the firm’s accounting and regulatory exports

**What it doesn’t do:**
- Doesn’t pay for itself below 50 active units — the investment is too high for the volume
- Still doesn’t replace human judgment on sensitive cases
- Doesn’t skip the litigation phase when it becomes necessary

**For whom:** firms with 80+ units, property managers who want to differentiate on service quality, or landlord groups pooling their management.

**Setup:** 6 to 10 weeks of design and development. Budget: 4,000 to 12,000 € depending on modules, plus 30 to 60 €/month hosting and maintenance.

## Traps to avoid

**Trap 1 — Automating the day-3 reminder with a bailiff’s tone.** This is the most common mistake when discovering automation. You turn on “automatic reminder”, leave the default template, and the tenant gets a dry email on the morning of the 4th. For someone who simply wired the money one day late, it feels like an attack. **The first email should feel like a nudge, not a threat.** “Hi, we haven’t seen your transfer come through yet — is there anything you’d like to tell us?” works ten times better.

**Trap 2 — Confusing automation with legal delegation.** No software can, or should, trigger a formal notice to pay or a legal summons on its own. Those steps are legal matters, vary by lease type and guarantees in place, and must go through a professional (lawyer, judicial officer). Your automation stops at the amicable phase — the rest you escalate.

**Trap 3 — Not planning a human “pause mode”.** A good tool must let you **manually suspend** reminders on a specific file without resetting everything. Example: a tenant calls to say her husband just died and she’ll settle within 15 days. If your system still sends the day-10 email because no one disabled the automation, you lose a client for ten years.

**Trap 4 — Forgetting GDPR consent and traceability.** Reminders sent must be tracked, timestamped and archived. It’s indispensable if a dispute later arises, and it’s also a GDPR obligation for tenant data. A serious tool does this natively; a spreadsheet-and-Word patch does not.

## Three questions to ask yourself

1. **How many hours per month do I actually spend on reminders?** Not the quick guess — the real number, cumulated across all files. If it’s more than 8 hours for fewer than 40 units, there’s automation to win starting tomorrow.

2. **When I send a reminder, do I reread the tenant’s human history?** If yes, you’re doing it right, but it isn’t scalable. If no, your tool isn’t helping you — or it doesn’t exist yet.

3. **If I were on sick leave for two weeks, would my files keep running?** The honest answer to this question says everything about how dependent your firm is on your personal memory. And therefore about its real fragility.
